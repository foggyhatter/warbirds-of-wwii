Supermarine Spitfire

The Supermarine Spitfire was the backbone of the R.A.F. The Spitfire first gained recognition in the Battle of Britain, where, despite being less robust than the Hawker Hurricane (the primary fighter in that battle), the Spitfire units earned a higher ratio in both victories to losses and kills to deaths. The Spitfire outperformed the weightier, more stable Hurricane in speed and maneuverability, but it was also hampered by its lesser weight, as the lighter wings were unable to efficiently store ammunition and .303 caliber machine guns of the Spitfire lacked the power of the Hurricane's 20mm cannons. In fact, some German fighters escaped practically unscathed since the .303 machine guns were unable to pierce the armor covering the critical parts of the Luftwaffe fighters.

Vought F4U Corsair

The Vought F4U and Goodyear's FG variant flew 65,000 missions for the U.S. Marines and Navy in World War II, accounting for 44% of all fighter missions flown by the Marines and Navy.

Over the course of the war, Corsair pilots were credited with 2,140 kills, compared to only 189 losses to enemy pilots. This left Corsair pilots with an outstanding overall kill ratio of 11:1. Against the Japanese Zero fighter, which was Japan's top naval aircraft since its introduction in 1940 and once considered the most capable carrier-based fighter, Corsair pilots scored a 12:1 kill ratio. With mastery of the Corsair, the U.S. gained air superiority in the Pacific theatre.

Yakovlev Yak-9

The Yak-9 was one of Russia's most-produced fighters, with nearly 17,000 units delivered.

The Yak-9 was used widely in Russia's campaigns. It performed better at lower altitudes, where many of the predominant German fighters flew. The Yak's main enemy, the Luftwaffe's Messerschmitt Bf 109, could not out-maneuver it, although the Yak possessed lesser weaponry. The Russians knew this of course, and so as one German put it: "The Yak was no match for the ME-109 but there were always so many...they swarmed like bees whenever we showed up" (warbirdalley.com)"
